# Delphinium — Marketing & Messaging Reference

A single source of truth for how Delphinium is positioned, what it does, who
buys it, and the language and proof points to use when writing about it.
Built for K-12 virtual and online schools.

**★ marks the highest-leverage points** — the lines, stats, and quotes that
do the most persuasive work and should be reused often.

## 1. Core positioning

### Headline

> ★ **Canvas delivers content. Delphinium delivers engagement.**

Supporting taglines used alongside it:
- **Do more, in less time**
- **Transform Canvas into an engagement engine**
- **Which class would YOU rather take?** (introduces the before/after
  comparison)

### Positioning statement

> For virtual and online K-12 schools running courses in Canvas, Delphinium
> is the Canvas engagement layer that turns an existing Canvas course into a
> visual, motivating experience for students and an early-warning system for
> teachers in about three minutes — unlike general LMS tools, which deliver
> content but don't address whether students want to engage with it, because
> Delphinium is built on published behavioral-science research and adds no
> extra work for teachers: they just turn it on.

### 25-word description

> Delphinium turns the Canvas courses virtual schools already have into
> engaging experiences for students and early-warning systems for teachers —
> with no extra work for teachers. Just turn it on.

Delphinium isn't "another LMS feature." It lives in the gap between content
delivery and student motivation — in the founder's words: *"We have all
kinds of tools to do lots of things for content... but we don't have a lot
of tools that help students want to engage. So that's what Delphinium was
designed from the ground up to do."*

## 2. ★ The proof point — "up to 31%"

> ★ **Cut failures by up to 31%.**

This is a measured result from one specific study — not a promise of what
every school will see. Always attribute it to the Davis study, and use "up
to 31%" in any forward-looking context (proposals, ads, pricing pages). The
unqualified "31%" is fine only when narrating the study's own result with
the study named.

**The Davis study (Davis Connect / Davis School District, Utah):**
- 72 fully online classes, 6,000 students
- Same courses, same content, same teachers, measured one year without
  Delphinium and the next year with it
- These were asynchronous classes where students never met their teacher or
  classmates in person
- Result: a 31% decrease in course failure rates

**Founder's framing (use this as the model):** *"We did a study where we had
72 classes, 6,000 kids... Turning on Delphinium, we were able to show a 31%
decrease in the failure rate. That's a big number. I don't know a lot of
tools that can post those kind of improvements."*

**Supporting survey data:**
- ★ 72% of students report Delphinium is "much more motivating" than a
  traditional Canvas course
- Asked how a Delphinium course is different, the single most common word
  students used was **"Fun."**
- ★ Student quote: *"I was sitting on the couch watching Netflix and the
  thought popped into my head: I could be doing homework right now."*

## 5. The behavioral-science foundation

What separates Delphinium from generic "gamification" — grounded in the
founder's academic background, which is itself a trust signal.

**Founder credibility:**
- PhD in applied social psychology; master's in educational technology;
  MBA
- Professor at Utah Valley University (organizational behavior)
- ~25 years in education; Delphinium has been in R&D since 2012, backed by
  14 years of published research (searchable on Google Scholar)
- ★ Origin story: *"Delphinium started in 2012 when I noticed that my own
  students seemed to be addicted to their smartphone games... I started
  thinking, I wonder if I can get them addicted to my homework."*

**★ The competition-vs-belonging insight** (keep this qualitative — no
specific percentage):
> Most students aren't actually motivated by competition. A small minority
> respond to leaderboards and ranking, but the majority respond far more to
> progress visibility, a sense of belonging, and intrinsic goal-tracking.
> Delphinium is designed for that majority, not just the competitive few
> most "gamified" tools default to.

This defuses the most common teacher objection ("we tried leaderboards and
kids hated it") and is differentiated positioning competitors don't have.

**The three research pillars (Delphinium's "tentpoles"):**
1. **Self-regulated learning** — students see at a glance where they've
   been, where they are, and where they're going, so they can set and
   monitor their own goals.
2. **Behavioral economics / nudge theory** — countdown timers (urgency),
   progress meters, prize boxes, and celebrations that pull students back
   into the course.
3. **Social presence theory** — "Who's Near Me" and shared avatars, because
   *"an online class is a really difficult place if you're a social
   person."*

## 6. Product capabilities

**★ The one thing to show first: the before/after.** A standard Canvas
course is a gray wall of text — the "gray scroll of death." Turning
Delphinium on transforms that same course, in about three minutes, into a
visual, color-coded, motivating interface. This side-by-side ("Which class
would YOU rather take?") is the single most persuasive moment in every demo
and the handout — lead with it whenever the format allows a visual.

### Student experience
- 3-minute Makeover: a gray wall of text becomes a visual, color-coded
  module view (green = done, blue = in progress, white = not started; with
  red/yellow/green quality bars showing work quality, not just completion)
- Course countdown timer — answers the #1 student question ("how much time
  do I have left?")
- Real-time performance meter (points earned vs. attempted) and
  volume-of-work meter ("you're 63% through the content")
- Gamification: avatars (near-infinite combinations), prize boxes (100+
  mini-games/unlocks), tiered alias-based leaderboards
- Works across desktop, tablet, and phone, and in the Canvas parent app
  (with a dropdown to switch between multiple children)

### Teacher experience — Control Tower
- One screen showing every student red/yellow/green — who's doing well,
  who's on the bubble, who needs help
- Click any student for full detail: grades per assignment, comparison to
  class average, comments, and a direct link into SpeedGrader
- Filter to struggling students and message all of them (with parents CC'd)
  in under a minute
- "Periscope" — reload the page as any student to instantly see what a
  parent or student sees
- Pulls live Canvas data; not AI-generated

### Teacher experience — Message Center
- Build once, reuse forever: teacher- or course-level templates that follow
  the teacher across sections and semesters
- Relative-date scheduling (e.g. "send 2 days before course start");
  recalculates automatically when a course is copied forward
- Conditional sending — rules like "only send if points < 50" or "only send
  if page views < 1 in the last 7 days"
- Multi-channel delivery from a single message: Canvas message, announcement,
  to-do list item, in-app banner, direct email — can fire simultaneously
- Add the teacher as a CC'd sender and/or a counselor as a silent recipient
  on at-risk messages
- 160-language auto-translation on outbound messages

### Setup & adoption
- ~3 minutes per course; the official walkthrough video is 2:23
- No extra work for teachers — Delphinium reads existing Canvas content
  live, so you just turn it on, and any later Canvas edit is reflected
  automatically
- Fully customizable layout: remove leaderboards, rearrange elements, build
  custom homepage tabs, embed HTML/links/slideshows
- Schools can set a standardized layout template so every teacher's 3-minute
  setup defaults to the school's branding and priorities

### Performance
- Delphinium's own footprint is minimal; the heaviest feature ("Who's Near
  Me") computes server-side and ships only a lightweight result — workable
  even on Chromebooks with constrained Wi-Fi.

Canvas resellers.

**What each persona cares about:**
- **District admin / IT / Assistant Superintendent:** ROI and time savings
  at scale, district-wide consistency, IT manageability (no new tools), data

  complaints, looking good to the district.
- **Teacher / end user:** not having to learn something new, getting
  Saturday nights back, students who actually engage, parent communication
  not being a burden.

## 8. Objection handling

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "We'd have to migrate all our Canvas content." | No migration needed. Existing courses become Delphinium courses in 3 minutes; ongoing edits sync automatically. |
| "Our teachers won't learn a new tool." | Nothing new to learn — teachers keep using Canvas exactly as before. Setup video is 2:23. |
| "We tried gamification before and students hated the leaderboard." | Only a small minority of students are motivated by competition; Delphinium's leaderboard is alias-based and tiered so students see only people near their own rank. Removable in one click. |
| "Is this FERPA compliant?" | Yes — student data stays within Canvas; leaderboards use aliases; no student PII leaves the Canvas environment. (Full technical detail lives in the separate security reference.) |
| "We already use Canvas well — do we even need this?" | Delphinium isn't for schools struggling with basic Canvas adoption — it's for schools that already have Canvas working and want to go from "functional" to "genuinely engaging." The Davis result happened at a school already using Canvas effectively. |
| ★ "Will messages actually reach students? They don't check Canvas." | Delphinium sends through multiple channels at once — Canvas message, to-do list item, in-app banner, and direct email — so there's no single point of failure. |
| ★ "Will this slow down our Chromebooks / hurt our Wi-Fi-limited students?" | Delphinium's footprint is minimal; the heaviest feature computes server-side and ships only a small result. Confirmed not to meaningfully add load. |
| "Is the Control Tower view printable?" | No dedicated print button today, but Ctrl+P captures the screen; open to scoping further with specific schools. |

## 9. Voice & tone for outreach

- Researcher, not salesperson — peer-to-peer, educator-to-educator
- Concrete and specific: real numbers, real situations, not generic claims
- Confident without being pushy; soft questions over hard pitches
- State the problem before the solution, every time
- Sign as **Jared, Chief Innovation Officer** (not "the Delphinium team")
- Never use: "I wanted to reach out," "hope this finds you well," "touch
  base," "circle back," "leverage," "synergy"
- Cold email body: 4–5 sentences max; one proof point only (usually the 31%
  stat, attributed); one soft CTA (a 20-minute demo, not a "sales call")
